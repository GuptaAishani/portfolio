Experience

AUG 2025 — PRESENT
FIREFLY AEROSPACE
Leander, TX
NASA Commercial Lunar Payload Services Intern

Working at Firefly since August 2025 really changed how I think about structural design. It’s one thing to see a part in a CAD model, but it’s another thing entirely to be in the cleanroom actually dealing with the physical constraints of aerospace bonding materials and weld quality. Those hands-on tasks like soldering harnesses and managing connector integration taught me that a design is only as good as its manufacturability. Supporting vibration and shock testing in the lab was also a massive reality check because you quickly realize just how brutal launch loads are on every single component. I walked away from this experience realizing that being a solid structures engineer isn't just about the initial design, it is about understanding the entire lifecycle of the hardware so you can catch integration risks before they ever reach the cleanroom.

SEP 2025 — PRESENT
TEXAS DESIGN BUILD FLY
UT Austin
Systems & Manufacturing Engineer

Working on the payload system for Design, Build, Fly was a massive lesson in spatial optimization and mass property management. I had to CAD-model a retention mechanism within a strict 4.5 inch by 4.5 inch fuselage cross-section, which meant hitting roughly 95% spatial utilization. The real challenge wasn't just fitting the hardware, but maintaining a consistent Center of Gravity across every flight envelope. Since our mission profiles were constantly changing, I spent a lot of time analyzing how payload placement and mass distribution shifted our stability margins, making sure the aircraft stayed balanced regardless of the configuration. This project also got me deep into the fabrication side. I performed carbon fiber and fiberglass layups using vacuum bagging and aerospace epoxy, where I had to optimize ply orientation to handle the specific structural loads we saw in testing. Seeing the assembly actually hold up during flight trials was the ultimate validation. It proved that my tolerances and alignment were solid under dynamic loading, but more importantly, it taught me how to bridge the gap between a high-fidelity digital model and a high-performance composite build.

AUG 2024 — PRESENT
TEXAS SPACE SOCIETY
UT Austin
Founder and President

Leading a multidisciplinary team of 12 for the Texas Space Society has been my biggest dive into the complexity of large-scale space design. We focused on the structural window and hull layering for lunar habitats, where I had to coordinate between our structures, materials, and manufacturing sub-teams to make sure our designs actually made sense from a cost and fabrication standpoint. It was a massive effort that resulted in a 30-page NASA-style engineering proposal in just three months, covering everything from load paths to modular layouts for scalable settlements.

While I love the deep-dive conceptual work of proposals, I’ve recently been shifting the club toward a more hands-on initiative. I’m currently leading VECTOR, a reusable, re-landing model rocket. It’s a huge shift for the organization, but I’m excited to apply those same structural and integration principles to hardware that we can actually launch, recover, and iterate on in real-time.

AUG 2024 — DEC 2025
TEXAS AERIAL ROBOTICS
UT Austin
UAV Manufacturing & Integration Engineer

I used Fusion 360 to design and assemble quadcopter airframes, focusing heavily on how to cut down component mass without sacrificing strength. I managed to drop the weight by about 15%, which directly translated to better flight efficiency and more room for payload. A big part of the project was sweating the small details, like producing a custom mounting bracket for the ESC controller. I had to maintain 10mm clearance tolerances to make sure everything sat securely within the electronics stack without interfering with other components. This project was really where I started to see the payoff of optimizing geometry for both performance and the actual assembly process.
